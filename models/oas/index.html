Option-Adjusted Spread (OAS)

Decomposes bond spread into option cost and residual compensation by simulating cash flows across rate paths.

P = (1/N) Σpaths Σt CFt / Π(1 + rs + OAS)
Key Insight: OAS strips out option cost to reveal pure credit/liquidity compensation -- the spread an investor truly earns after accounting for embedded optionality.

When Spreads Diverge: OAS vs Z-Spread vs Nominal

For a bullet bond (no embedded options), all three spread measures converge. The differences emerge when optionality is present:

  • Nominal Spread simply subtracts one yield from another. It ignores the shape of the yield curve and any options.
  • Z-Spread properly accounts for term structure by using the full spot rate curve, but still assumes deterministic cash flows (no optionality).
  • OAS goes further: it simulates cash flows across many rate paths (Monte Carlo), allowing prepayment or call exercise to vary by path. The resulting spread reflects only credit and liquidity risk.

The gap between Z-Spread and OAS equals the option cost: the compensation investors lose to the embedded option.

Callable Bond Example:

A corporate callable bond trades at 98.50 with a 6% coupon. The issuer can call at par after 3 years.

  • The nominal spread might be 180 bps
  • The Z-spread might be 165 bps (correcting for curve shape)
  • The OAS might be only 120 bps
  • Option cost = Z-Spread - OAS = 45 bps

The investor appears to earn 165 bps over Treasuries, but 45 bps of that compensates for the call option the issuer holds. The true credit/liquidity compensation is only 120 bps.

For MBS, the option cost can be much larger because prepayment optionality is exercised more frequently and less predictably than corporate calls.
